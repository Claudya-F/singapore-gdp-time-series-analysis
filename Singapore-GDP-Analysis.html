Singapore GDP Growth Time Series Analysis

Author

Claudya Fong

1. Introduction

This project analyses Singapore’s GDP growth using time series methods in R, including STL decomposition and ARIMA/ETS modelling. The objective is to understand trend behaviour, evaluate model suitability, and produce short-term forecasts.

3. Exploratory Analysis

GR |> autoplot(GDP) +
  labs(
     y = "Growth Rate (%)",
     x = "Time (Quarter)",
  title = "Singapore GDP Growth Rate"
  )

Since the series is YoY-based, seasonality is expected to be reduced, which aligns with the weak seasonal component observed.

Overall, Singapore’s GDP growth rate shows a mild downward trend over time. Several sharp declines are visible during periods of economic disruption, most notably around 2020 Q1 during the COVID-19 shock.

lambda <- GR |> features(GDP, guerrero) |>
  dplyr::pull(lambda_guerrero) 
GR |> autoplot(box_cox(GDP, lambda))

To assess whether variance-stabilising transformation was needed, the Guerrero method was applied. The suggested Box-Cox parameter was 1.174, which is close to 1, indicating that transformation is not necessary.

dcmp <- GR |>
  model(stl = STL((GDP) ~ season(window = "periodic"),
                  robust = TRUE))
components(dcmp) |> autoplot()

The STL decomposition shows a mild downward trend over time. The seasonal component is weak and relatively stable, which is consistent with the expectation that a YoY series contains limited seasonal variation. The remainder appears largely random, suggesting that most systematic movement has been captured.

4. Modelling

Fit <- GR |>
  model(
    auto_ARIMA = ARIMA((GDP), stepwise = FALSE, approx = FALSE), 
    auto_ETS = ETS(GDP)
    )

ARIMA is more suitable as the series shows weak seasonality and relies primarily on autocorrelation structure rather than trend smoothing.

This is supported by the model selection criteria. ARIMA appears preferable because it provides a better trade-off between model fit and complexity than ETS. Proven by lower AIC, AICc, and other indicators.

Fit |>

The residuals do not appear normally distributed and show left skewness. Clear outliers are also visible around 2020 Q1, likely reflecting the COVID-19 shock.

Although ARIMA has the lowest AIC, Ljung-Box results indicate residual autocorrelation, suggesting model inadequacy and potential for improvement.

Therefore, the selected ARIMA model should be interpreted with caution, as its residuals still contain structure not fully captured by the model.

5. Forecasting

EGR <- GR |> 
  model(
    auto_ARIMA = ARIMA((GDP), stepwise = FALSE, approx = FALSE))|> 
  forecast(h = 24, level = c(80, 95))
EGR|>
  autoplot(GR) +
  labs(
     y = "Growth Rate (%)",
     x = "Time (Quarter)",  
     title = "Singapore GDP Growth Rate") + 
  coord_cartesian(xlim = yearquarter(c("2010 Q1", "2030 Q1")))

The forecasts suggest a short-term rebound in growth followed by stabilization, indicating cyclical recovery rather than sustained long-term acceleration.

6. Conclusion

The analysis indicates a gradual slowdown in GDP growth with significant disruption during COVID-19. While ARIMA provides the best fit among the models considered, diagnostic results suggest remaining autocorrelation, indicating potential for further refinement. Forecasts suggest short-term recovery followed by stabilization.
